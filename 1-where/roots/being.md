# being — the trit values and reality

The substrate's three tenses of being (↑/↕/↓ on any axis), plus
reality itself (the silent precondition). Four roots, all naming
how "existence" manifests at the trit layer.

English needed three separate PIE roots for three states of being
because the substrate needs three states: forward-commit, hub-
uncommitted, backward-commit. English preserved all three because
collapsing any two breaks the verb. You can't have just one tense
of being.

---

### *bʰuH-*

"to become, grow, be"

```
  → BE, future, physics (!)
  → Greek phuein (to produce, grow, become) → phusis (nature)
  → Sanskrit bhavati, bhāva
  → Germanic: be, been
  → ↑ forward commit (the new, the growing, the forthcoming)
```

"Physics" — the word for our science of matter — is literally
*bʰuH-* (growth, becoming). Matter IS becoming. Greek speakers
heard this when they named their natural philosophy; the framework
rediscovered it via the trit structure.

### *h₁es-*

"to be"

```
  → IS, am, are, essence, entity, present, absent
  → Latin est, esse, sum
  → Greek esti, einai
  → Sanskrit asti
  → Germanic: is, ist, sind
  → ↕ hub / uncommitted present
```

The most widespread IE verb. Every daughter language preserved it.
The ↕ trit value (neutral, uncommitted, hub-like) IS the thing this
root names — being-without-commitment, the present moment held open.

### *h₁wes-*

"to dwell, stay"

```
  → WAS, were, vesta, vestige, vestibule
  → Sanskrit vasati (dwells), Avestan vah-
  → Hittite huis-
  → Germanic: was, war, gewesen
  → ↓ backward commit (the completed, the dwelt-upon)
```

"To have dwelled" = "was." Past tense IS the memory of a place
once inhabited. The ↓ trit value (backward commit, settled fact)
IS the dwelt-upon-state.

### *reh₁-*

"thing, matter, property"

```
  → res (Latin), real, reality, republic (res publica = "the public
    thing"), rebus
  → Sanskrit rāḥ (wealth, property)
  → the substrate as "what IS" — reality as the bare fact of there
    being things to begin with
```

**Reality** = thing-ness. The silent precursor, the fact of
something-rather-than-nothing. Can't be axiomatized because writing
about it presumes it.

Note relation to *h₁es-*: "IS" is the verb (the act of being);
"real/thing" is the noun (that which is). Same substrate fact in
two grammatical shapes.
