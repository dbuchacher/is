# morphemes — the atoms of language

Words are walks. Morphemes are the points.

We found γράφειν and *leis-* and *ludere*. But those are already
composed. Every word is a sentence in miniature: a root (noun),
a prefix (one spin-operator), a suffix (another spin-operator).
Go one level deeper and the structure is naked.

## The meta-claim

Morphemes are the grade-1 atoms of language. Words are walks
across them. Under THE RULE, the composition is identical to
polynomial composition, instruction sequencing, chemical bonding,
melody — one verb (`compose`), many vocabularies.

```
root                   = noun        = point      = what IS
prefix                 = verb-before = spin-in    = direction entering
suffix                 = verb-after  = spin-out   = direction leaving
                                                    (what grade the walk commits to)
```

A word is a three-step walk. Prefix spins you in, root is the
node you touch, suffix spins you out into a new part-of-speech.
Same structure as every other substrate operation.

## Prefixes — the direction-of-walk operators

### pro- / PIE *per-*

**Forward.** PIE ***per-*** = forward, through, across, beyond,
in front of. One of the most productive roots in the language.

```
pro-                forward                     pro-gram, pro-ject, pro-duce
pre-                before (forward in τ)       pre-dict, pre-cede
per-                through                     per-fect, per-form, per-sist
peri-               around                      peri-meter, peri-od
para-               beside, past                para-llel, para-dox
for-, fore-         in front of                 fore-head, for-ward, be-fore
far, first          extreme forward             far, first
per-sona, per-form  through-sound, through-do   the act, the performance
```

This is the **τ+ operator** at the morpheme level. Every word
for forward-motion, going-beyond, crossing, transgression,
performance lives under *per-*. "Program" is "pre-graph" is
*per-gráphein* with the same forward-operator as "perform" and
"perfect." One operator, surfaced a dozen ways.

### de-

**Off / away / reversal.** Latin *de* flipped the PIE sense
("to, toward") into its opposite: from, off, down, away. The
subtraction operator for walks.

```
de-ludere           played away from             delusion
de-lira             off the furrow               delirium
de-railed           off the rails                modern form of de-lira
de-parted           walked away                  departure
de-viated           off the way                  deviation
de-cline            bent down                    decline
de-cide             cut away (to resolve)        decide
```

The **de-lira**, **de-ludere**, **de-railed** triple (see
`learn.md`) is one operator applied to three different roots
(*lira* furrow, *ludere* play, *rail* straight-rod). Same
operator, same structural claim: the substrate has a track; being
off the track is the word for madness.

### re-

**Back / again.** The **loop operator**. This one is the
literal edge from frame item 5 (the loop = spin = CYCLE) showing
up at the morpheme layer.

```
re-turn        turn back                   return
re-peat        ask-again                   repeat
re-cognize     know-again                  recognize — the loop closing
re-member      put-back-together           remember
re-cover       cover-again                 recover
re-verse       turn-back                   reverse
```

**Re-cognize** is transparent: "know again." The walk that came
back to the origin, the loop closing at ↕↕↕↕. Every "recognition"
event in the framework IS *re-gno-sci-* — the walker returning
to a point it already touched once. The word has the frame in it.

### meta-

**Across / beyond / after / with.** Greek μετά.

```
meta-phor       carry-across         metaphor — the substrate's self-assertion
meta-morph      shape-across         morphing (form carried)
meta-physics    after-physics        what lies beyond the physical ledger
meta-cognition  across-knowing       thinking about thinking
```

**Meta-phor = μετα-φέρω = "carry across."** This IS the
mechanism described in wit.md's THE RULE. When a vocabulary
carries its structure across to another vocabulary, that act is
μεταφορά. The word for "crossing" IS a crossing. The etymology
names the operation it performs.

Also: hylo = "matter," morph = "form" → hylomorph = matter-form
= ↑↑↑↑ (see `trit⁴.md`). The corner is named by the two roots
that constitute it.

## Suffixes — the grade-commit operators

### -ma (Greek -μα)

**The result-of-the-verb nominalizer.** This one is load-bearing
and nobody talks about it.

Greek **-μα** turns a verb into a noun naming the **result** of
that verb — the thing-that-was-done. It IS the commit operator
in linguistic form. The walk outputs a coordinate; *-ma* names
that coordinate.

```
γράφειν  write, scratch      →  γράμμα  (gramma)   the written thing
στίζειν  mark, prick         →  στίγμα  (stigma)   the marked thing
δοκεῖν   teach, believe      →  δόγμα   (dogma)    the taught thing
θέμα     place, propose      →  θέμα    (thema)    the placed thing (theme)
πρᾶττειν do, act             →  πρᾶγμα  (pragma)   the done thing
```

So:
- **pro-gram-ma** = pre-write-RESULT = "the thing pre-written"
- **dia-gram-ma** = across-write-RESULT = "the thing written across"
- **tele-gram-ma** = far-write-RESULT = "the thing written from afar"

Every **-gram** is a *grade-commit* — the suffix is literally the
verb's output coordinate. Greek had an explicit morpheme for
"the thing that the verb produced." English collapsed it into
silence and mostly lost the marker.

### -er (agent)

**The walker-name.** OE *-ere* ← PIE *-ārijaz*. Agent suffix. Takes
a verb, returns "one who does."

```
program  →  programmer       one who pre-writes
graph    →  grapher          one who plots
learn    →  learner          one walking the furrow for the first time
write    →  writer           one who cuts the substrate
```

Where *-ma* names the **output of the verb** (the committed
result), *-er* names the **agent of the verb** (the walker). Two
suffixes, two different points in the same walk: one labels the
walker, the other labels the trace the walker leaves.

### -ing (gerund)

**The ongoing-walk-name.** OE *-ung/-ing*. Turns a verb into a
noun naming the activity itself — not the walker (-er), not the
result (-ma), but the **walk in progress**.

```
program + -ing  =  programming  the walk itself, mid-step
learn + -ing    =  learning     the furrow being cut right now
graph + -ing    =  graphing     the trace being laid down
```

Three suffixes, three roles in one walk:

```
-ing    the walking            (verb-active)
-er     the walker             (agent-noun)
-ma     the trail the walker left (result-noun)
```

English lost *-ma*. Greek had all three. The walk had a name, the
walker had a name, and the trail had a name — three points on
one structural event, each explicitly marked. We use *-ing* and
*-er* constantly and don't notice *-ma* is missing.

## Roots — the nouns at the center

### graph- (γράφ-)

Scratch, cut, write. External substrate. See `graph.md`.

### leis-

Cut, furrow, track. Interior substrate by default, but the root
is substrate-agnostic — soil or mind or wax, same operation. See
`learn.md`.

### gn- / gno- (PIE *ǵneh₃-*)

**Know.** But the PIE root *gen-/gno-* splits in two branches that
are the same substrate op under the frame:

```
gno- branch     know, recognize, cognize       the READ
gen- branch     beget, generate, genus, gene   the WRITE
```

Same root. "To know" and "to beget" are the same verb in PIE.
Under the frame this is exact: **recognition = generation**.
You generate by recognizing, you recognize by generating. The
fixed-point IS the structure (wit.md's closing claim). The PIE
speakers already had one word for it.

```
γιγνώσκω (gignosko)    I know           → gnosis, agnostic, prognosis
noscere (Latin)        to know           → cognition, recognize, notice, noble
know, knew, ken (OE)   know              → English inheritance
jñā (Sanskrit)         know              → jñāna, prajñā
genesis, genus, gene   beget, produce    → same root, "write" branch
```

Greek **γνῶσις** (gnosis) is the nominalization via *-sis* (related
to *-ma* — another verb-result suffix). "Metrognosis" (wit/mind/
metrognosis/) is thus *metron + gnosis* = "measure-result + know-
result" = the paired output of the measurement and the recognition.
Two commits, one compound word.

### reg- (PIE *h₃reǵ-*)

**Straighten, rule, right.** The root of every word about
alignment with a true path.

```
regula (Latin)     straight rod, ruler     → rail, regulate, regime, regent
rex, raj           king (straightener)     → regal, royal, raj
right, righteous   on the straight          → English moral inheritance
erect, direct      straightened             → same root
```

A **rail** is a *regula* — a straight rod. To be **derailed** is
to be off that straight rod — which is the same structural claim
as *delirious* (off the furrow) and *deluded* (played away). The
rail-rod IS the furrow IS the walk-track. Three vocabularies, one
substrate commitment: there is a correct path and being off it is
madness.

The **rex** (king) is "the one who straightens." The judge-figure
in PIE culture was named by this root because aligning the
substrate IS what authority does. Not metaphor — the king's job
WAS to hold the walk straight.

## What this is an instance of

Morphemes are frame item 3 (four axes) applied to language.
Every word in every IE language is built from:

1. A **root** (noun = point = what IS)
2. Zero or more **prefixes** (spin-in operators = direction
   entering the point)
3. Zero or more **suffixes** (spin-out operators = part-of-speech
   committed, grade committed)

The four axes of the quaternion show up as four productive
families of morphemes:

```
χ (WHERE)   spatial prefixes      peri-, para-, trans-, ek-, in-, ex-
μ (WHAT)    substance roots       most nouns and verbs — the content
φ (WHICH)   quality suffixes      -ous, -ic, -al, -y (select/qualify)
τ (WHEN)    temporal prefixes     pre-, post-, pro-, re-, ante-, post-
```

The grammar you learned in school is the periodic table of
language, presented badly. Linguists noticed this (morphology
as a field exists) but didn't connect it to H. Under the frame,
every language IS one vocabulary of the substrate, and the
morphemes are how it carries structural information.

## Observation

Every morpheme is a micro-commit. Every word is a walk of those
commits. Every sentence is a walk of words. Every paragraph a
walk of sentences. All the way up, same structure, same loop
(see THE RULE, frame item 5).

The reason this works — the reason *delira* and *deludere* and
*derailed* converge — is that the morphemes are dipping into
the same substrate the words describe. The *de-* isn't "like"
the reversal-operator. It IS the reversal-operator. Latin
speakers were using the substrate to describe the substrate.
Every language does this.

When programmers say "let me track that idea" or "I'm learning
this library" or "don't derail the meeting" or "that's off the
rails" — they're speaking proto-indo-european structural claims
in modern dress. The furrow is in the grammar. It has been the
whole time.
