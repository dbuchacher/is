# PIE morphemes — organized by date of the concept

PIE itself was spoken ~4500-2500 BCE on the Pontic-Caspian
steppe. The **roots** all existed in the same language during
that window. But the **concepts** the roots name have different
ages — some go back to the Paleolithic era before agriculture,
some emerged with farming ~10,000 BCE, some were invented during
the PIE era itself (wheel, metals, kingship).

Organizing by date of concept gives a chronological layer-cake of
human substrate-recognition **before writing existed**. Each
layer is what humans had to name at that point in their history.

PIE is the DEEPEST RECOVERABLE window we have into English's
ancestral language, and the ~500 morphemes below are the most
confident reconstructed set. Anything older than PIE is either
speculative (Nostratic, proto-human) or unrecoverable.

Notation:
- `*` = reconstructed form
- Laryngeals `h₁`, `h₂`, `h₃` = real PIE consonants, lost in most daughters
- Subscript numbers on vowels = distinct variants of the same phoneme

# LAYER 1 — Paleolithic Universals (~100,000+ BCE)

Concepts every human had since anatomically modern humans
existed. These roots name things that have been part of human
life for 100,000+ years — before agriculture, before cities,
before writing. When PIE was spoken, these concepts were already
ancient beyond memory. They are the **oldest stratum** of named
substrate.

# LAYER 2 — Neolithic (~10,000-4500 BCE)

Concepts that emerged with the **agricultural revolution**. When humans started farming and herding, they needed new words. PIE inherited these from the Neolithic era that preceded it; they are roots for things that didn't exist in Paleolithic life.

## 2a. Domesticated animals

These were all domesticated during the Neolithic. Their names in PIE reflect the new relationship with animals as property.

| *gʷōus | cow / cattle | **cow, bovine,** beef, Latin *bos*, Greek *bous* |

# LAYER 3 — Bronze Age / PIE-specific (~4500-2500 BCE)

Concepts that emerged **during the PIE era itself**. These roots specifically date PIE — if PIE has a word for X, X existed when PIE was spoken. The wheel, wagon, and metallurgy roots below are the strongest dating evidence we have: PIE must be more recent than the invention of these things.

## 3a. The wheel complex — PIE's smoking gun

These roots all come from the **wheel package**: wheel + axle + yoke + wagon. All invented ~3500 BCE. Their presence in PIE dates PIE to post-3500 BCE.

| Root | Gloss | Descendants |
|---|---|---|
| *kʷékʷlos | wheel | **wheel,** cycle, Sanskrit *cakra*, Greek *kuklos* |
| *h₂eḱs- / *h₂eḱs-lo- | axle / axis | **axle, axis,** Latin *axis*, Greek *axōn* |
| *yugóm | yoke | **yoke, conjugal,** junction, Latin *iugum*, Greek *zugon*, Sanskrit *yuga* |

**These five roots are all in PIE.** They are not in any earlier reconstructable layer. They lock PIE's date: PIE speakers lived after the wheel was invented. This is how we know PIE existed ~4500-2500 BCE and not 8000 BCE — no wheel words means no wheel concept means pre-PIE; all IE daughters have these words means late PIE had them.

## 4c. Inflectional morphology — case, number, person

PIE had:
- **8 noun cases**: nominative, accusative, genitive, dative, ablative, locative, instrumental, vocative
- **3 numbers**: singular, dual, plural
- **3 genders**: masculine, feminine, neuter
- **Verb categories**: person (1/2/3), number (s/d/pl), tense (present/aorist/perfect), aspect (imperfective/perfective/stative), mood (indicative/subjunctive/optative/imperative), voice (active/middle)

**Core endings** (representative):
- Nom sg: *-s
- Acc sg: *-m
- Gen sg: *-os, *-es, *-s
- Dat sg: *-ei
- Verb 1sg: *-mi (present) / *-m (past)
- Verb 2sg: *-si / *-s
- Verb 3sg: *-ti / *-t
- Middle voice: *-h₂er (1sg), *-soi (2sg), *-to/-or (3sg)

Sanskrit preserves these most completely. Greek next. Then Latin, Old Slavic, Old English. Modern English dropped nearly all of them — only the genitive *-s* (*'s*) and third-person *-s* (*he runs*) survive from PIE inflection.

# Summary

**Morpheme count by layer:**

- Layer 1 (Paleolithic universals): **~200 roots** — everything pre-agricultural
- Layer 2 (Neolithic): **~40 roots** — agricultural / settlement vocabulary
- Layer 3 (Bronze Age / PIE-specific): **~20 roots** — wheel, metals, hierarchy, abstract concepts
- Layer 4 (grammatical apparatus): **~40 items** — prefixes, suffixes, case endings, verb morphology

**Total confident morpheme inventory: ~300**

**What each layer contains:**

The Paleolithic layer (1) is the **oldest substrate-contact vocabulary available to English**. Body, family, sky, fire, water, beast, eat, sleep, walk, see, know, say, think, love, fear, good, bad. This is what humans have been naming for 100,000+ years. When PIE speakers used these words, they were already ancient. The concepts were unchanged from the earliest modern humans — only the specific phonetic forms shifted.

The Neolithic layer (2) records the **agricultural revolution** — what had to be named after ~10,000 BCE. Grain, plow, livestock-as-wealth, house, settlement. These are the first named artifacts of apparatus-civilization.

The PIE-specific layer (3) records the **Bronze Age** — wheel, wagon, axle, yoke, metal, king. These roots lock PIE's historical date. A language without them is older than PIE; a language with them is PIE or descended from it.

The grammatical apparatus (4) is the **language's own structure** — the machinery PIE used to build words out of roots.

## What this is — in a line

**This list is what English's ancestral vocabulary looked like before anyone wrote anything down.** Every English word either descends from these ~300 morphemes (most native words, plus Latin/Greek/French loanwords that also descend from PIE), or is a loanword from a non-IE language.

**Reading this list is reading the pre-writing substrate-contact vocabulary of our own language.** The morphemes above are the oldest recognition events English can trace. Deeper than this is speculation (Nostratic, proto-human). This layer is solid science — the comparative method applied to ~450 daughter languages, working backward from the written records to the pre-literate source.

**The list is the substrate's own dig through itself.** 300 morphemes, no writing, full live carrier. The Paleolithic mind named what it saw. We inherited the names. The apparatus then buried them under 10,000 years of institutional freezes, but they are still in every English sentence you speak — you just can't see them without digging.

This is the dig.
