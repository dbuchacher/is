## Why does anything move? — Thermodynamic answer

If balance is the attractor, why isn't the universe already at
perfect ↕↕↕↕? What "unbalances" things in the first place?

The four laws of thermodynamics give a parallel answer set:

```
  law              implication for motion
  ───              ──────────────────────
  0th transitive   the balance attractor is unique (one ↕↕↕↕)
  1st conservation flows must net to zero (can't lose stuff,
                   only redistribute)
  2nd entropy↑    motion has direction (toward equilibrium /
                   maximum entropy)
  3rd T→0,S→0     perfect balance is unreachable in finite steps
                   — absolute zero unreachable per 3rd law
                   (with caveats — see below)
```

The 3rd law says perfect ↕↕↕↕ (absolute zero, perfect
equilibrium) is **asymptotically unreachable**. The system can
approach balance arbitrarily closely but never sit at it. There's
ALWAYS some residual imbalance, generating motion (rebalancing
toward ↕↕↕↕), which by being motion never lets the system fully
settle.

### 3rd law caveat — not iron-clad

The 3rd law isn't strictly proven the way 1st or 2nd are. It's
**empirical generalization (Nernst 1906) + quantum-theoretical
support**, with known approximate violations:

- Glasses, some magnetic materials with degenerate ground states
  retain residual entropy as T → 0
- Water ice has ~3.4 J/mol·K residual entropy from proton
  positioning
- Strictly: 3rd law is "approximately true in nice systems," not
  iron-clad universal
- Sometimes called "Nernst's principle" rather than "law"

Empirically: nobody's hit absolute zero (cooled atoms to ~10⁻¹² K
with extreme effort), trend supports asymptotic unreachability.
Convergent evidence, not mathematical proof.
