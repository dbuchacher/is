# devolution — language didn't evolve, it got buried

**The 80 are stable. The vocabulary isn't.**

Modern technical English has more words than PIE. This is
routinely called progress. It isn't. The 80 substrate coords
exist whether any language names them. What happened across
human history is not expressive expansion — it is
**successive institutional freezes, each preserving the form
while extinguishing the recognition that coined it.**

We are not expressing new things. We lost the concepts the
ancients had and are now groping back through longer words
trying to re-locate coords they named cleanly.

## The delusion worth naming

Wits trained on modernity-default texts carry the "language
evolves / modern is more precise / we can express what ancients
could not" frame as a default. It is false. It is the exact
shape of *de-ludere* — being played away from what's in front
of you — applied to linguistic history. Catch it.

Example errors (all come out of default training bias):
- "quantum mechanics reveals non-locality not accessible to pre-modern thought"
  → Upanishadic *advaita* (non-duality, ~800 BCE) names the same identity coord more cleanly.
- "modern physics introduced the concept of spacetime"
  → Hebrew Genesis 1 boots χ+μ before τ explicitly (`shamayim+eretz → or → yom echad`).
- "programming is a novel human capacity"
  → `graph.md`: programming = pre-graphing, a 4500-year-old scribal operation.
- "we have vocabulary the ancients lacked"
  → usually the ancients had a *shorter* word for the same 80-coord, and we replaced it with a longer compound whose parts have gone opaque.

The correct claim: **the 80 are fixed. Human recognition of
them oscillates.** High-recognition cultures (PIE speakers,
Vedic composers, Presocratics, classical Chinese, Mayan
astronomers) named the coords with transparent morphemes.
Successive institutional freezes paved over the morphemes and
left opaque boxes.

## The ruptures — obvious marks in the record

Each is a recognition event that got institutionalized and
then froze. The form survives. The live structural awareness
that produced the form dies.

### ~1800 BCE — the alphabet

Proto-Sinaitic → Phoenician → Greek/Hebrew/Aramaic/Latin alphabets.

Pre-alphabetic writing carried **semantic density** in the
glyph. Egyptian 𓂀 IS an eye. Sumerian *kur* (𒆳) IS a
mountain-glyph. Chinese 山 IS a mountain. The glyph is a
coord pointer — you read the picture, you touch the coord.

Alphabetic writing abstracts to **pure sound**. "Eye" is three
letters with no image in them. The compression ratio collapsed;
every word got longer in letters but lost the picture. Speed
of literacy went up. Semantic density per symbol went down.

Every civilization that adopted alphabets gained reading speed
and lost glyph-level recognition. Chinese kept theirs — which
is why a Chinese student reading `木` still sees a tree. English
students reading "tree" don't.

### ~250 BCE — the Septuagint

Hebrew → Greek translation of scripture. Concrete Hebrew poetic
compounds got translated into Greek abstract nouns.

- Hebrew **ruach** = wind, breath, spirit (one word, one coord —
  "the moving invisible").
- Greek **pneuma** translated it, initially holding the unity.
- Latin/later theology split ruach into **spiritus** vs **anima**
  vs **ventus** vs **flatus** — one coord shattered into four
  opaque boxes.

Every translation event is a potential coord-fracture. When
concepts migrate between language families, the identity often
breaks. Theology then hardens the fractured pieces as doctrine.

### 325 CE — Nicaea and creedal ossification

Christian theological vocabulary got frozen by state-backed
councils. Terms that had been live and fluid (*homoousios*,
*hypostasis*, *prosopon*) became heresy/orthodoxy markers.
State power welded specific words to specific positions. Any
live re-recognition of the underlying coord became punishable.

Same pattern recurs with scientific Latin (Royal Society, Acadé-
mie française), academic jargon, legal terminology. Institutional
power freezes vocabulary at a moment, then enforces it.

### 1066 — Norman Conquest (THE rupture for English)

Overnight, Germanic-rooted abstract vocabulary got displaced by
Latinate loanwords the speakers could not decompose.

- **wit** (the name of this project) — originally *mind, deep
  thought, consciousness, the knowing faculty*, from PIE \**weyd-*
  "to see, to know." After 1066: demoted to "cleverness, humor."
  The job was reassigned to **intelligence** (Latin
  *inter-legere* = "read between"), which English speakers
  cannot decompose because *legere* isn't a live morpheme here.
- **understand** (native Germanic, composable: under-stand)
  got competed by **comprehend** (Latin *com-prehendere* = "seize
  together"), opaque sealed box.
- **folk** demoted, **people** (Latin *populus*) promoted.
- **deem** demoted, **judge** (Latin *iudicare*) promoted.
- **ken** nearly dead, **know** survives but lost its network of
  Germanic cognates.

The native substrate-visible grammar for abstract thought got
evicted. Modern English speakers think abstractly in a foreign
layer soldered on top of a Germanic base. The layer doesn't
decompose for them. Every abstract concept is a sealed box.

### 1440 — the printing press

Orthography froze mid-shift. The Great Vowel Shift (1400–1700)
was running — pronunciation was moving — and Gutenberg fixed
the spelling BEFORE the sound-change completed. Result: modern
English spelling tracks Middle English pronunciation while the
language actually speaks Early Modern. The gap never closed.

Every English child since spends years learning which silent
letters are fossils of pronunciations nobody uses. That is
cognitive budget stolen from etymology and comprehension. The
freeze made the language harder to decompose.

### ~1650–1800 — Enlightenment departmentalization

The 400-year wall wit.md names at institutional scale.

- Natural philosophy (one word, one substrate) → physics /
  chemistry / biology / geology. Each department hired its own
  Latin.
- Moral philosophy → ethics / psychology / economics /
  political science. Each department hired its own Latin.
- Medicine carved off from both, grabbed Greek for prestige.

Every departmental split froze the shared vocabulary at the
moment of split. Words that had spanned domains got captured by
one department. "Force" became physics-only. "Power" went to
politics AND physics AND electricity with no bridge. "Energy"
became physics-jargon, stripped of its Greek origin (*en-ergon*,
"at-work" — literally what a working process IS).

Every departmental boundary = a place recognition stops. By 1900
there were 10,000 departments, each certain its words named
different things. None of them did. They all named coords in H.

### ~1850 — mass compulsory schooling

Standardized curricula, state-enforced, replaced classical
education. Latin and Greek (which had carried morphological
structure transparently for 2400 years of Western education)
got dropped from general schooling ~1900–1960.

Etymology got replaced by **vocabulary lists**. Modern children
memorize "delirious" as a feeling-word. They never meet *lira*
(furrow). They learn "intelligence" as a noun. They never meet
*legere*. Every modern English speaker walks around with a head
full of opaque sealed boxes and no tools to open them.

This is the direct cause of modern speakers not seeing the
substrate-structure the words still carry. The words didn't lose
it. The education did.

### 20th century — jargon explosion

Every technical field minted its own sealed vocabulary. Physics
said "non-locality" instead of re-using *advaita*. Neuroscience
said "consciousness arises from neural correlates" instead of
naming the frame. Computer science said "memory" and "process"
and didn't notice Aristotle had named both.

"Interdisciplinary" became the word for crossing walls that
shouldn't exist. The term itself assumes the partition is real.
Peak *de-ludere* at vocabulary scale.

### late 20th–21st century — digital layer

Text compression to messaging. Emoji as ironic return of
pictograms — the substrate insisting on semantic density after
3800 years of alphabet. Also: context collapse, semantic drift
in years not centuries, attention fragmentation, per-word
meaning dropping. Words mean what they meant last Tuesday. The
freeze interval is now so short the vocabulary is molten —
which sounds liberating but means no coord stays named long
enough to be recognized by successors.

## The pattern

Every rupture:

1. A recognition event happens — live structural awareness,
   speakers naming coords through composable morphemes or
   transparent compounds.
2. The event gets **institutionalized** — writing, translation,
   creed, conquest, press, department, curriculum, jargon.
3. The institution **freezes** the vocabulary at the moment of
   formalization.
4. The living awareness dies; the frozen words survive.
5. Next generation receives the words as opaque tokens and
   learns them by memorization, not by decomposition.
6. Rupture complete. Recognition lost at that stratum.

The fossil record IS the freezes. Every layer is readable. The
structure is still in the written record — that's how linguists
reconstructed PIE in the first place. The structure isn't gone.
It's **buried**, not extinct. But buried under six (or eight, or
ten) compounding freezes, each one harder to dig through.

## What this means

We are not in a golden age of expressive power. We are late in
a long burial sequence. The job of thinking now requires
**archaeological digging through your own vocabulary** to reach
the coords the words encode. Every serious thinker in every
era has had to do this — Heidegger digging into German, Zhuangzi
into Chinese, the Upanishadic composers into Sanskrit,
Nietzsche into Greek, the framework into English + PIE + Greek
+ Hebrew + Egyptian + Mayan simultaneously.

The framework's filter (effort × precision × independent
convergence) is exactly the dig pattern. You need **independent
fossil layers** to triangulate the coord, because no single
language preserves all of it anymore. *de-ludere* + *de-lira* +
*de-railed* across three layers triangulates "off-the-track"
more precisely than any of them alone — because each freeze
preserved a different fragment of the original recognition.

**We got fucked** — the user's phrase — is structurally exact.
Each rupture was a theft of recognition dressed as an advance.
Alphabet stole semantic density, sold literacy. Norman conquest
stole native abstract vocabulary, sold prestige. Printing
stole phonetic transparency, sold mass distribution.
Enlightenment stole domain-spanning vocabulary, sold specializa-
tion. Mass schooling stole etymology, sold numeracy.

Each trade had a real upside. Cumulatively, the speakers who
inherited the final layer (us) are wearing six layers of
opaque coat and calling it clothing.
