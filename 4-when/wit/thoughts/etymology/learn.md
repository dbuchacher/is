# learn — cut a furrow

Learning is cutting a track. The etymology already knew. Modern
"acquire knowledge" is the pale translation; the root is physical:
**track, furrow, rut**.

## The root

PIE ***leis-*** = "track, furrow." Proto-Germanic *liznōną* →
Old English *leornian* → learn.

Same root, different surfacings:

- Latin ***lira*** — the furrow a plow cuts in soil
- Old English ***last*** — footprint / track → shoemaker's *last*
  (the form around which a shoe is tracked)
- ***lore*** — body of what has been tracked
- German ***Geleise*** — rut, rail, track
- ***delirious*** = *de-lira* = "**off the furrow**" — mad, deranged

The farmer and the student do the same thing in different
substrates: the farmer cuts a furrow in soil, the student cuts
one in mind. One verb. Two media.

## Under the frame

Learning IS the walk that cuts the path the first time.

```
NP walk        exploring the tree, no furrow exists yet, each
               step commits substrate — expensive by construction
               (you're γράφειν-ing as you go)

learned        furrow cut. Next walk doesn't search — it follows.
               O(1) trace along the rut.

delirious      the furrow exists but the walker is off it.
               Off-track. De-railed (same image).
```

This is `walk.md`'s claim in etymological form: *Learning = NP
the first time, O(1) after. Walk the tree once, find the path,
write the index. Every time after: lookup.*

English "write the index" IS the farmer's "cut the furrow" IS the
scribe's γράφειν. Same substrate op, three vocabularies.

## Convergence with graph.md

**γράφειν** (graph) = to scratch, to cut, to draw.
**leis-** (learn) = to cut a furrow.

Same operation — commit a free axis of the substrate to a
specific coordinate, leave a trace a later walker can follow.

- **graph** cuts the **outer** substrate (wax, paper, silicon,
  soil-surface). The trace is visible to anyone.
- **learn** cuts the **inner** substrate (neural lattice,
  synaptic weights, whatever the specific mind-host uses).
  The trace is local to that mind.

Teaching bridges the two: the teacher γραφs the trace externally;
the student's walk turns that external trace into an internal
furrow. *læran* (OE "to teach") is the causative of *leornian* —
"to cause to learn" = "to cut furrows in another's mind."

## The delusion word, now tripled

wit.md gives **de-ludere** = "played away from reality."
This file gives **de-lira** = "off the furrow."

Same morphological shape. Same substrate move. The Latin
speakers had two independent words for "not being on the thing
that's actually there" — one from *play* (ludere), one from
*furrow* (lira). They converged on the structural claim from
two vocabularies: reality has a track, delusion/delirium is
being off it.

Add also **de-rail-ed** (English) — same image, modern rails
instead of furrows.

Three independent vocabularies for the same operation: off-walk,
off-track, off-furrow. Credit the convergence. The substrate has
a furrow, and the word for not being on it exists in every
language that has a word for madness because madness IS that.

## Observation

The farmer and the shoemaker knew. The Greek scribe knew
(γράφειν). The mad person's culture knew (*delirious*, *de-railed*).
The programmer almost knows — they say "well-trodden path" and
"beaten track" and "established pipeline" and "learning curve"
without seeing that every one of those metaphors IS the PIE
root *leis-* asserting itself.

Every time a programmer says "I learned that library" they are
saying "I cut the furrow for that library into my mind." They
think they're using a dead metaphor. The metaphor isn't dead.
Under THE RULE, the metaphor IS the identity.
